# Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) — Foundational Knowledge

> **Reference**: https://docs.cdpi.dev/

## What is DPI?

Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) refers to shared digital systems that function like public goods — open, interoperable, and available to all actors in an economy. DPI is distinct from government IT systems or private digital platforms: it is population-scale infrastructure that enables an entire ecosystem of services to be built on top.

DPI is the digital equivalent of roads, electricity grids, and telecommunications networks: the government (or a public body) builds and governs the foundational layer, and the private sector builds on top to create value.

## The Core DPI Model: Public Rails, Private Innovation

The defining characteristic of DPI is the separation between the **infrastructure layer** (the rails) and the **application layer** (the trains):

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│            APPLICATION LAYER (Private)               │
│  Banks │ Fintechs │ Health Apps │ AgriTech │ EdTech  │
│  Government Services │ NGOs │ Startups │ MNOs        │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│            DPI RAILS (Public)                        │
│  Identity Auth │ Payment Switch │ Data Exchange      │
│  Open APIs │ Open Standards │ Public Governance      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**The rails model delivers two simultaneous outcomes:**
1. **Public sector scale**: Governments can deliver services to entire populations at low cost using DPI rails
2. **Private innovation**: The private sector can build on the same rails to create competitive products — a fintech using the same identity and payment rails as a government welfare scheme

This "build the rails, not the train" principle prevents government from needing to build every application, while also preventing any single private actor from monopolizing foundational infrastructure.

## The DPI Three-Layer Stack

DPI is organized around three foundational building blocks:

### 1. Identity (Who are you?)
- Verifiable digital identity for individuals and entities
- Population-scale enrollment and authentication
- Open authentication API usable by any licensed service
- Examples: National ID programs, federated identity systems, OIDC-based authentication gateways

### 2. Payments (Can you transact?)
- Interoperable instant payment rails open to all licensed participants
- Account-to-account transfers, merchant payments, government disbursements
- Examples: Instant payment switches, interoperable mobile money, QR-based payment standards

### 3. Data Exchange / Consent (Can you share data?)
- Consent-based, auditable sharing of personal data across organizations
- Enables data portability and citizen data empowerment
- Examples: Open banking APIs, federated health data exchange, consent management frameworks

## Core DPI Principles

> **Reference**: https://docs.cdpi.dev/the-dpi-wiki/dpi-tech-architecture-principles

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Open & Interoperable** | Open standards, open APIs — any compliant party can participate |
| **Federated** | No single point of failure or control; distributed architecture |
| **Inclusive by Design** | Works for last-mile populations, low connectivity, diverse literacy levels |
| **Privacy-preserving** | Minimal data, consent-based, purpose-limited, anti-surveillance design |
| **Public good orientation** | Non-extractive; governed in public interest, not for profit |
| **Reusable** | Each layer enables countless use cases on top |
| **Country/context ownership** | The jurisdiction governs and owns its DPI stack |
| **Minimalist** | DPI is the thinnest possible layer that enables maximum reuse |

## DPI vs. GovTech vs. Digital Platforms

| | DPI | GovTech | Digital Platform |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Who builds** | Public/multi-stakeholder | Government | Private company |
| **Access** | Open APIs for all | Often closed | Terms-of-service |
| **Business model** | Public good | Tax-funded | Profit-seeking |
| **Portability** | Built-in by design | Rare | Rare |
| **Lock-in** | Designed to prevent | Varies | Designed to create |

## Why DPI Matters

### Speed at Scale
DPI enables governments to deliver services to entire populations in months rather than decades. Countries with mature DPI stacks disbursed COVID relief payments in 48-72 hours; countries without took months.

### Financial and Service Inclusion
Open authentication APIs allow the private sector to onboard customers who were previously excluded due to high KYC costs. Interoperable payment rails enable last-mile access through any licensed provider.

### Cost Reduction
DPI shifts the cost curve for public services dramatically:
- Identity verification: from $5-25 per check → $0.01-0.50
- Payment disbursement: from 5-7% transaction cost → near-zero
- Document verification: from manual days-long process → seconds

### Innovation Multiplier
Each DPI layer multiplies private sector innovation. An interoperable payment rail does not just enable one payment app — it enables hundreds, all competing on service quality rather than on owning the rails.

### Resilience and Anti-Monopoly
Federated, open architectures prevent single actors (public or private) from achieving monopoly control of foundational infrastructure. DPI is structural competition policy.

## Global DPI Landscape

DPI has been deployed across diverse contexts:

| Region | Identity Examples | Payment Examples | Data Exchange Examples |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **South/SE Asia** | National biometric ID programs, OIDC gateways | Instant payment switches, QR standards | Open banking, consent frameworks |

## DPI Design Sequence

Most successful DPI programs follow this logic:

```
1. Anchor in identity — establish who someone is
2. Enable payment — let them transact
3. Empower with data — let them control and share their information
4. Build registries — authoritative records of entities, credentials, relationships
5. Issue credentials — portable, verifiable proofs of status
6. Open to ecosystem — private sector and civil society build on all of the above
```

## The DPI Governance Imperative

DPI infrastructure is powerful and sensitive. Governance is not optional:

- **Separation of roles**: Regulator (sets rules) ≠ Operator (runs system) ≠ User (builds on system)
- **Accountability**: Clear lines of responsibility; grievance mechanisms for citizens
- **Oversight**: Independent bodies; civil society participation
- **Privacy**: Anti-surveillance design embedded in the architecture, not just in policy

DPI built without strong governance can become surveillance infrastructure or monopoly power for the operator. The governance layer is as critical as the technical layer.
